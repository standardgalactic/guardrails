The Moon Should Not Be a Computer

The paper "The Moon Should Not Be a Computer" argues against relocating AI infrastructure to space, suggesting instead that computation must be integrated into terrestrial ecosystems. The author introduces xylomorphic computation, a framework where AI systems are designed to recycle their own thermal and material byproducts, effectively turning "waste" heat into a resource. By applying the xylomorphic order parameter (λ), the text demonstrates that orbital compute projects are fundamentally unstable because they lack the ability to reintegrate energy and information. This lack of closure is mirrored in cognitive development, where over-reliance on AI can prematurely fix a learner's reasoning within a narrow attractor basin of the model's own making. Ultimately, the source advocates for dual closure, requiring that large-scale AI maintain both thermodynamic efficiency and epistemic independence from its users. Stable scaling is only possible for systems that operate in a contractive regime, treating every output as a substrate for future growth.
